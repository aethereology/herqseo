# QueryClear strategic brief: build the operator, not another dashboard

> **Note (2026-07-11):** the agent substrate changed after this brief was written — Hermes Agent was dropped (never installed) in favor of the **Claude Agent SDK** behind the same `AgentRuntime` interface (decision D14 in `memory.md`). References to Hermes below are historical; the strategy itself is unchanged.

**The decision is to build QueryClear as a true autonomous SEO/AEO/GEO operator for mid-market B2B SaaS — priced as a $2,500–$15,000 one-time setup plus a $599–$3,499/month subscription, explicitly positioned as the agent that *executes* the work the entire $300M-funded GEO category only *measures*.** This wedge exists because every well-funded competitor — Searchable, Profound, Bluefish, Peec, AthenaHQ, Scrunch — sells dashboards and recommendations, while only Relixir and Daydream credibly execute, and neither targets the segment where willingness-to-pay is highest and product fit is cleanest: the 3–15-person marketing team at a Series A–C B2B SaaS company who can't afford a $15K Daydream retainer and won't survive on a $295 Profound dashboard. **The first 18 months are about owning that wedge, not chasing Searchable into the enterprise.** What follows is the case, the numbers, and the plan.

## The competitive picture is clear and the gap is real

Searchable.com is a credible, well-capitalized competitor — but not the threat it appears. Founded in 2025 by Chris Donnelly (Verb Brands, Lottie) and Arya Nagabhyru, it raised **$18M total ($14M Series A in May 2026 at an $85M valuation, led by Headline)**, claims **$2M ARR within 4.5 months** of its January 2026 launch, and lists AmEx, KPMG, Pfizer, Siemens and BCG as customers. Pricing is **$50 / $125 / $400 per month plus enterprise**, with four product modules (Agent, Analytics, Content, Audit) covering ten AI engines, plus a new "Actions" task layer and an MCP server for Cursor/Claude Code. The founder pitches it as "the first Autonomous SEO and AEO Growth Engineer" and "execution layer, not another dashboard."

**That last claim is the vulnerability.** Independent reviewers (Orchly.ai, the broader practitioner community) characterize Searchable as a chat-based co-pilot with recommendations and prioritized tasks — *not* an executor. The most damning evidence is structural: Searchable's "Actions" feature exports work to Linear for humans to do. By contrast, **SearchAtlas's OTTO actually applies on-site changes via a JavaScript pixel**, and **Relixir's Rex autonomously researches, writes, and publishes content end-to-end**. Searchable is closer to Profound-with-content than to a true agent. Donnelly's own framing — "agentic co-pilot trained on your brand…works alongside your team" — confirms it.

The broader landscape sorts into three layers. **Pure GEO/AEO visibility platforms** ($29–$5,000/mo) are dominant — Profound ($155M+ raised, ~$1B valuation, $99–$5,000/mo), Bluefish ($68M raised, Fortune 500 only), Peec AI ($29M, $95–$495/mo), AthenaHQ (YC, $295–$595/mo), Scrunch ($19M, $250–$1,200/mo), Otterly ($29–$989/mo), Goodie AI, BrandRank.AI, and Searchable itself. **Legacy SEO platforms with AI bolt-ons** — Semrush One ($199–$549/mo), Ahrefs Brand Radar ($828–$1,148/mo all-in), SE Ranking ($103–$240/mo), Similarweb, Surfer, Writesonic — own incumbent distribution but feel retrofitted. **AI-native services** — Daydream ($21M raised, ~$15K/mo retainers, $70M ARR with <20 staff) and Relixir's Pro tier — are the closest to true autonomy but operate as boutiques.

**The universal complaint, repeated across Reddit, LinkedIn, analyst reviews, and even competitors' own marketing, is that current tools "tell you you're invisible but not what to do."** Profound itself acknowledged execution was secondary to visibility. Kevin Indig, Rand Fishkin, Lily Ray, and eMarketer all converge on the same critique: **the category is over-supplied with dashboards and under-supplied with operators.** That is the opening.

## The market is large enough, growing fast enough, and disoriented enough to enter now

The global SEO services market is **~$89B in 2024 heading toward $144B by 2030**, with software at $74B → $155B. The GEO/AEO subsegment is the fastest-moving slice: estimates range from **$848M to ~$2.5B in 2025**, projected at 45–50% CAGR to roughly $17–34B by 2034. More importantly, the demand signal is now undeniable: **94% of CMOs plan to increase AEO investment in 2026** (Conductor), **88% of marketers have already begun optimizing for AI responses** (Salesforce State of Marketing 2026), and **37% of marketing teams now track AEO as a dedicated KPI, up from 9% in early 2025**.

The pressure forcing those budgets is brutal and well-documented. **Ahrefs measured a 58% CTR drop on the #1 ranking page when AI Overviews appear**, up from 34.5% in April 2025. Seer Interactive measured **−61% organic CTR and −68% paid CTR** on AIO queries — but brands *cited inside* an AIO earn 35–91% *more* clicks. Pew measured 8% click-through with AIO vs. 15% without. Gartner predicts traditional search volume drops 25% by 2026 and 50% by 2028. Meanwhile ChatGPT reached **900M weekly active users by Feb 2026**, Perplexity hit ~$200M+ in revenue, and AI referral traffic is growing **527% year-over-year** from a 1.08% baseline (Conductor, 3.3B sessions).

This is the rare moment where the customer's problem ("our funnel is shrinking and we don't know how to appear in AI answers") is visceral, the budget is being approved at the CMO level, and the incumbent solutions (Profound, Searchable, Ahrefs Brand Radar) leave a clear "now what?" gap. **The whitespace is not better measurement — it is execution that closes the loop.**

## The recommended ICP is mid-market B2B SaaS, with agencies as the second wedge

I am explicitly choosing **mid-market B2B SaaS companies, Series A through C, $5–$50M ARR, with marketing teams of 3–15 people**, as the beachhead. The reasoning is mechanical, not aspirational. B2B SaaS already sees **11–18% of discovery from AI engines** (Digital Applied 2026) — the highest of any vertical — which means urgency and willingness-to-pay are both high. These companies have outgrown Otterly and HubSpot's free grader but cannot stomach Profound's $499–$5,000 ramp or Daydream's $15K retainer. They have a Head of Growth or VP Marketing with decision authority and a 30–60 day sales cycle, not the 6-month enterprise procurement Bluefish and Profound now require. They already pay Semrush or Ahrefs and feel those tools are bolt-ons. They don't have a dedicated SEO hire, much less an "AEO specialist," so a done-for-you autonomous agent removes a hiring problem they can't solve.

The **second wedge is marketing agencies managing 10–50 client brands** — currently underserved on multi-client workspaces (only Scrunch and Peec address this competently), with strong word-of-mouth dynamics and per-client expansion ACV. **A deliberate Agency Partner tier with white-label, per-client billing, and pitch workspaces becomes the channel multiplier** by month 9.

I am explicitly **rejecting** three tempting targets: **Fortune 500 enterprise** (Bluefish/Profound are entrenched, 6-month sales cycles burn cash QueryClear doesn't yet have, and Searchable already owns marquee logos QueryClear would have to displace one-by-one); **pure SMB/solopreneurs at <$100/mo** (unit economics break — SE Ranking's 2025 agency survey shows 75% dissatisfaction below $500/mo, CAC eats LTV); and **DTC e-commerce** as the launch vertical (interesting given AthenaHQ's Shopify wedge and 805% YoY AI traffic growth, but requires platform-specific integration that delays MVP — better as the Year 2 expansion vector).

## QueryClear should be a true operator, not another dashboard

The product must do four things end-to-end, autonomously, with humans-in-the-loop only at approval gates. **First, the agent monitors brand visibility, sentiment, and citation share across at least eight AI engines** (ChatGPT, Google AI Overviews, AI Mode, Gemini, Perplexity, Claude, Copilot, Grok — with Rufus and Meta AI added by month 12) and reconciles signals with GA4 + Google Search Console + CRM data to attribute AI visibility to pipeline and revenue. **Second, it generates and publishes content** — answer-first structured pages, FAQ schema, comparison pages, technical glossaries — directly into the customer's CMS (WordPress, Webflow, Contentful, Sanity, Shopify) with brand-voice training from ingested customer materials. **Third, it executes on-page technical fixes** via a CMS plugin or, where feasible, a JavaScript injection pixel modeled on SearchAtlas OTTO — schema markup, internal linking, llms.txt curation, AI crawler accessibility, meta optimization. **Fourth, it runs a continuous off-page citation campaign** — prompt-gap detection, Reddit/YouTube/Wikipedia opportunity surfacing, automated outreach for citation placement.

The single hardest design decision is **how autonomous to make it.** My recommendation is a configurable autonomy slider with three default modes: **"Review"** (agent proposes, human approves every action), **"Auto-publish"** (agent executes within pre-approved guardrails — schema, internal links, content under 1,500 words on approved topics — and escalates anything outside the box), and **"Autopilot"** (full execution with weekly review summaries). Most mid-market B2B SaaS buyers will start in Review and graduate to Auto-publish within 60–90 days. **This is the explicit differentiator vs. Searchable's task-export model and Profound's pure-monitoring posture.** The marketing line writes itself: *"Searchable tells your team what to do. QueryClear does it."*

The product must have **explainable methodology** — every recommendation traces to a specific prompt, engine, and source document, surfaced in the UI — to counter the "vanity dashboards" critique that has dogged Profound and is the single biggest user complaint in the category. **And it must integrate natively with GA4, GSC, HubSpot, Salesforce, Shopify, and at least three CMSes from day one.** AthenaHQ wins customers on Shopify revenue attribution; QueryClear should ship with the deepest attribution graph in the mid-market.

## Technical architecture: one dedicated Hermes agent per customer, model-agnostic brain

The build rests on a deliberate architecture decision: **every customer gets their own dedicated, persistent agent instance, built on Hermes Agent** (the open-source autonomous agent framework from Nous Research, MIT-licensed, released February 2026). Hermes is the right foundation because it provides exactly the primitives this product needs and that a stateless chatbot wrapper cannot: **persistent cross-session memory** (the agent remembers each brand's site, voice, history, and what's worked), **a self-improving skills system** (it builds reusable optimization playbooks from experience), **scheduled autonomous execution** (the cron-based monitoring-and-publishing loop that *is* the core product), and **40+ built-in tools** including web search, browser automation, and code execution. It self-hosts on infrastructure as cheap as a $5 VPS or idle-cheap serverless (Modal, Daytona), which keeps per-customer infrastructure cost negligible.

**The reasoning "brain" is the OpenAI API, not a ChatGPT subscription — and this distinction is non-negotiable.** A consumer ChatGPT Plus/Pro subscription is a UI product with no programmatic API and terms that prohibit reselling it inside a commercial product; building QueryClear on per-customer consumer subscriptions would be technically unworkable and a ban-risk. The commercial path is the metered OpenAI API under business terms. Critically, **Hermes is model-agnostic** — it connects to the OpenAI API, OpenRouter, or any endpoint — so QueryClear routes reasoning across GPT-class, Claude, Gemini, and open models per task to optimize cost and quality. **This is itself the primary mitigation for the platform-dependency risk: QueryClear is never hostage to a single provider's pricing or policy changes.**

On top of Hermes sits QueryClear's proprietary layer — the orchestration, multi-tenancy, billing, brand-safety guardrails, the autonomy slider, and the CMS/GA4/GSC/CRM integrations. That layer, not the open-source agent underneath, is the defensible IP.

## Unit economics: metered tokens are the main variable cost

The architecture decision reshapes the cost model. With a dedicated agent per customer running 24/7 across eight engines, **metered API tokens become the dominant variable cost** — and an uncontrolled autonomous agent will quietly destroy margin. This is precisely why the setup fee and the per-tier caps on prompts and published content exist: they are not arbitrary packaging, they **meter each customer's token consumption** and keep the unit economics honest. The target is **70–80% gross margin after API costs**, enforced by per-customer token budgets that scale with tier (100 prompts on Operator, 2,000 on Scale). Model-agnostic routing protects this margin further — cheaper models handle monitoring and classification, frontier models handle content generation and strategy. The setup fee then covers the token-heavy onboarding burst (full-site audit, brand-voice training, baseline measurement) so it doesn't bleed into subscription margin.

## Pricing: setup fee plus subscription, anchored on agency replacement

The pricing structure should explicitly mirror **what mid-market companies already pay agencies**, because that is the buyer's mental model and the comparison that justifies the spend. The SE Ranking 2025 agency survey shows mid-market retainers cluster at $4,000–$12,000/month with **setup fees of 50–100% of monthly retainer** as the industry convention.

| Tier | Setup fee | Monthly | What's included | Target customer |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Operator** | $2,500 | $599 | 1 domain, 5 AI engines, 100 prompts, 8 published content pieces/mo, GA4+GSC, Review mode | Seed/Series A SaaS, in-house solo marketer |
| **Growth** | $5,000 | $1,499 | 3 domains, 8 engines, 500 prompts, 25 pieces/mo, full integrations, Auto-publish, CRM attribution, dedicated CSM | Series A–C SaaS, marketing team of 3–10 — the core ICP |
| **Scale** | $10,000 | $3,499 | Unlimited domains, 10+ engines, 2,000 prompts, 75 pieces/mo, Autopilot, API, SSO, SOC 2, quarterly strategy | Series C+ / pre-IPO, 10–25 person teams |

This structure does several things at once. It **prices below Profound's enterprise tier and Daydream's $15K retainer** while sitting comfortably above the $295 dashboard mid-market and the $828 Ahrefs all-in cost — landing squarely in what the pricing research called the "lonely middle" of $500–$2,000/month where competition is sparse. It **front-loads cash via setup fees** (15–25% of ACV, matching the SaaS norm) which funds the heavy onboarding work — site audit, brand voice training, integration, baseline measurement — that an agency would otherwise bill standalone for $3,000–$5,000. **The setup fee filters tire-kickers** (customers who pay setup show 2–3x retention versus pure free-trial converts) and funds CAC payback in 6–9 months at Growth tier, well inside the 12-month SMB benchmark. And it **reserves usage-based overage** (per published piece, per audit, per additional engine) as the natural expansion lever — modern AI agents from Devin to Fin.ai have proven this is what mid-market finance teams will tolerate when paired with a predictable base.

I considered and rejected three alternatives. **Pure subscription** (the Searchable/Profound model) underprices the high-touch onboarding that an autonomous agent uniquely requires and lengthens CAC payback to 18 months. **Pure outcome-based pricing** (per AI citation, per ranked page) is intellectually attractive but operationally fatal at this stage: Sparktoro's research shows AI responses are non-deterministic — **<1 in 100 chance the same prompt returns the same brand twice** — making outcome guarantees a legal and operational minefield. **Pure done-for-you services** (Daydream's $15K model) caps growth at the rate you can hire strategists and dilutes SaaS multiples; QueryClear should *replace* services, not become one.

## Go-to-market: content-led, founder-sold, agency-channeled

The first 90 days are about **founder-led sales to 20 mid-market B2B SaaS design partners** at the Growth tier, with setup fees discounted 50% in exchange for case-study rights and product feedback. Inbound channels should be sequenced: **founder content on LinkedIn and X** (the GEO/AEO conversation lives there, not in trade press), **a free AEO audit tool** (mirror Searchable's try-agent and HubSpot's AEO Grader as a top-of-funnel demand magnet — every competitor has shown this works), **two original research reports per quarter** (the SparkToro/Profound playbook — original data is the single most-cited GEO tactic and earns its own AI citations), and **a deliberate AEO Conference sponsorship + Mike King iPullRank co-marketing** to establish category credibility.

The agency channel opens in month 6 once the core product is stable, with a **20% recurring revenue share** to agency partners and a dedicated white-label dashboard. Agencies are the highest-leverage distribution in this category — Searchable, Otterly, Peec, and AthenaHQ have all gone there — and QueryClear has a structural advantage because the agent actually does the work agencies bill for, making the economics genuinely compelling rather than just additive.

## Risks are real but manageable with explicit mitigations

**Platform dependency is the existential risk.** OpenAI, Google, Anthropic, and Perplexity can change their crawling, citation, or API access rules without notice — and have. Mitigation: the **model-agnostic Hermes architecture** means no single model provider can hold the product hostage on price or policy; beyond that, instrument multi-engine redundancy from day one, contribute to industry standards efforts (llms.txt, schema.org extensions), maintain server-side scraping fallbacks where APIs degrade, and refuse to make the product depend on any single engine for >30% of value.

**The "GEO is just SEO" critique is the marketing risk.** Rand Fishkin and Ann Smarty have publicly argued GEO/AEO is rebranded SEO with noise added; SparkToro's <1-in-100 reproducibility study is the strongest skeptical evidence. Mitigation: lean into it. Position QueryClear as a unified **SEO + AEO + GEO + analytics operator** rather than a GEO-only tool, so the value holds even if the GEO category collapses into a feature.

**Competition from incumbents is the strategic risk.** Semrush One ($199–$549/mo, launched October 2025) and HubSpot's $50/mo AEO product can bundle aggressively. Mitigation: **execution-first positioning** is structurally hard for incumbents to copy because it requires re-architecting around an agent runtime, not a dashboard. Searchable will likely add execution within 12 months; QueryClear needs a 9–12 month head start on real autonomy and integration depth.

**Capital intensity is the financial risk.** Profound raised $155M+, Bluefish $68M, Daydream $21M, Peec $29M, Searchable $18M — the table stakes are high. QueryClear should target a **$3–5M seed round on the strength of design-partner traction**, focused on the mid-market wedge story rather than enterprise ambitions. The economics work: at $5K average ACV (Growth tier annualized) and 12-month CAC payback, $3M of capital funds ~600 customers and a path to $3M ARR within 18 months — comparable to Searchable's pace at a fraction of the burn.

**Methodological credibility is the trust risk.** SparkToro's reproducibility research and Lily Ray's "lofty promises" warning will follow any GEO startup. Mitigation: publish methodology transparently, show confidence intervals on every metric, and credit "validation layer" thinking (per Kevin Indig) in the product UX.

**Token cost runaway is the margin risk** (new, introduced by the per-customer agent architecture). Autonomous agents running continuously can burn API tokens far beyond plan economics. Mitigation: hard per-customer token budgets enforced by tier, model-agnostic routing that sends cheap tasks to cheap models, caching of repeated queries, and overage billing that turns excess consumption into revenue rather than loss.

## Execution roadmap: ship the operator in 90 days, prove the wedge in 180

**Months 0–3 — Build the agent's core loop.** Stand up the Hermes-based per-customer agent with OpenAI API as the default brain. Ingest a domain, audit it across 5 AI engines, generate 10 prioritized opportunities, draft and publish 5 pieces of content through a WordPress + Webflow integration, and measure citation lift after 30 days. Single CMS, single vertical (B2B SaaS), single autonomy mode (Review). Recruit 10 design partners at Growth tier with discounted setup. Goal: **$50K of paid setup revenue and 5 customers in active publishing by day 90.**

**Months 3–6 — Add Auto-publish, GA4/GSC/HubSpot/Salesforce integrations, and expand engine coverage to 8.** Implement model-agnostic routing and per-customer token budgets. Onboard 20 paying Growth customers, hit $30K MRR. Hire VP Engineering and head of customer success. Launch free AEO audit tool as inbound funnel. Begin agency conversations.

**Months 6–12 — Launch Agency Partner tier and Autopilot mode.** Add Scale tier with SSO and SOC 2 Type I. Expand to Shopify integration for DTC pilot. Target **$1.5M ARR, 80+ paying customers, 5–8 agency partners.** Raise Series A on a clear category-leading metric: published-content velocity per customer 5–10x higher than Searchable's claimed output.

**Months 12–18 — Defend the mid-market wedge and begin selective enterprise motion.** Add custom-model fine-tuning per brand, on-prem options, regulated industry compliance. Target **$5M ARR, defensible CAC, NRR >120%, and a clear seven-figure ACV pipeline from displaced agency retainers.**

## What changes if I'm wrong

The plan has three failure modes worth pre-committing mitigations for. If **autonomous execution proves too unreliable** (CMS API breakage, hallucinated content, brand-safety incidents), QueryClear pivots to a "human-supervised agent" model at Daydream-like pricing — still differentiated by the per-customer agent runtime, just slower. If **GEO/AEO collapses into a Semrush/HubSpot feature**, the unified SEO+AEO operator positioning still holds because the agentic execution layer is the moat, not the GEO category. If **mid-market B2B SaaS proves harder to penetrate than agencies**, flip the wedge — lead with the Agency Partner tier and let agencies pull QueryClear into their mid-market customers.

**Searchable is a credible competitor but a beatable one because they've taken the dashboard-with-tasks position, not the operator position.** Profound, Bluefish, and Peec are racing toward enterprise. Daydream owns the high-end agency replacement. The mid-market B2B SaaS company with a 5-person marketing team, a shrinking organic funnel, a CMO mandate to "win AI search," and no $15K/month retainer budget — that buyer has no good option today. **Build for them. Charge them like a junior agency replacement. Execute their work autonomously with a dedicated, model-agnostic Hermes agent per customer. Win the next twelve months on speed and integration depth, and the category will sort itself around the company that actually does the optimization rather than the one that visualizes it.** The market is large, the urgency is real, the incumbent weakness is documented, and the pricing math works. Build the operator.
